→ WHAT THIS IS

The mockup at benwwtf.github.io/stolen-years is not a video or a click-dummy: it is working software with a live shared backend. This document maps it for your engineers, so you can judge quickly what is worth keeping, what you will replace, and where the genuinely unsolved work is.

Everything described here is handed over without restriction. Treat the code as a foundation or as a reference implementation to discard; both are fine with us. It was written to be read: vanilla ES modules, no build step, no framework, comments where intent isn't obvious.

One rule survives any rewrite: no tracking, no analytics, no cookies, and no donation amounts stored anywhere. Privacy is part of the concept.

03 / 08

→ THE SCENE

Visual grammar (this is the load-bearing part)

  • The lived line: white, a comet trail: dark in the far past, brightening toward the present. A pulse of light travels along it in every beat; the line is alive before anything happens to it.
  • The sick timeline: empathy beige #d4b896, continues from the break, droops, and fades to black at its tip. This is "the stolen years" themselves. The word stolen in the copy carries the same color.
  • The hope fan: three faint blue strands. Crucially, they rise from a point on the sick branch, not from the break: research does not undo the infection, it bends the sick timeline upward from where the patient actually is. A donation ignites the middle strand (#2d6be4 → #bcdcff); non-monetary actions ignite in lime (#c5e866).
This anchoring decision (hope sprouts from the sick line) came out of the team's own review and is deliberate. If you change it, change it knowingly: it is the sentence the geometry speaks.

How the reveal works

All colors live in per-vertex buffers under additive blending, so color × 0 is invisible. The staged reveal across the nine beats is therefore pure color math on one geometry; no objects are added or removed, ever. Ignitions blend a branch from its dormant color to its lit color over 1.4 s; a per-life drift ("breathing") and the hero pulse are written into the same buffers each frame.

Performance posture

  • Mobile: fewer lives (140), thinner lines (1.7 px vs 2.2 px), softer bloom (0.65 vs 0.85), stronger pulse and drift so the small frame stays alive.
  • Bloom is reduced when the field is dense (the beat-4 stream, the finale): more glow over many lines reads as grey fog; less glow keeps strands distinct.
  • Camera, reveal, and rotation all derive from one lerped scroll value read inside the rAF loop: no scroll handlers, no scroll-jacking.

05 / 08

→ INTERACTION GRAMMAR

The prototype commits to a two-verb grammar. It is the emotional architecture of the piece and we ask you to preserve its meaning even if you redesign every surface of it:

Destruction is a tap

Instant, casual, almost accidental: that is how the disease arrives. At beat 02 the scroll story pauses on an intact line and a quiet prompt (touch the line). One tap anywhere breaks the timeline.

Soft gate: scrolling past breaks it automatically; the story can never stall. Under reduced motion the reveal stays scroll-driven and the copy is never hidden.

Creation is a hold

The submit button charges for 2 seconds under a sustained press; release early and the charge drains back. Illness takes a second, repair takes commitment: the visitor's own fingers learn the difference.

Keyboard (Enter) and reduced-motion users submit instantly. An aborted press teaches the gesture: “Press and hold until it ignites.”

The ignition cinematic (the payoff)

On a completed hold the page briefly takes the camera away from the scroll: the form fades out, the camera chases the traveling light up the branch (donor name riding alongside as a CSS2D tag), rests on the newly lit timeline while the share clip finishes recording (~9.4 s total), then auto-delivers the visitor to the share beat. If the visitor scrolls during the sequence, control is theirs; we fall back to a toast. Camera scroll-control and follow-mode share one lerp state, so handoffs never snap.

Ambient reactivity

  • Desktop: the whole scene leans gently toward the cursor (pointer parallax on the camera target).
  • The field breathes constantly (per-life sinusoidal drift); the hero pulse runs in every beat and continues through the fork into the lit branch after ignition.
  • Your brief asks for much more here (ripples, bending toward touch): agreed, and nothing in the architecture blocks it: all positions live in one dynamic buffer that is already rewritten per frame.

→ THE LIVE LAYER

The galaxy is shared. Every ignition persists, every visitor sees all previous lights, and new ignitions appear in real time while you watch; the page is never the same twice.

ComponentBehavior
Table ignitionsid · name · dedicate · kind (gift|action) · action · created_at. No amounts are ever stored. Anonymous lights store name = null.
SecurityRow Level Security: public read, constrained public insert, no update/delete. The publishable key in the client is public by design; the RLS policy is the actual boundary.
Loadon page load, latest 400 ignitions light their branches instantly (settled, no animation); total count comes from an exact count query.
Realtimeone WebSocket subscription on INSERT: someone else donates, a branch ignites live in your open tab.
Placementignition id hashes deterministically to a life in the galaxy: the same light occupies the same branch for every visitor, which makes personal links meaningful.
Share links?light=<id> greets the arriving visitor with the donor's light by name ("Anna's light is part of this galaxy").
Counterglobal campaign total; deliberately hidden below 10 so early days don't read as empty. Every 10th ignition bends the whole structure for a breath.
Offlineif Supabase is unreachable the page runs fully local; ignitions still animate, nothing errors.
07 / 08

→ THE SHARE CLIP

After an ignition the visitor gets a real vertical video of their own moment, not a canned asset. The WebGL canvas is center-cropped into a 720×1280 compositing canvas at 30 fps, the donor overlay ("I lit a branch." · name · dedication) is drawn on top, and ~9 seconds are recorded via MediaRecorder: MP4 on Safari, WebM on Chrome/Firefox, ~1-2 MB. Because the ignition cinematic follows the light, the clip is automatically well-framed.

  • Share clip hands the file to the native share sheet (Web Share API Level 2): on iOS/Android this includes Save Video, the road into the photo library and into stories.
  • Download clip saves the file directly; browsers without MediaRecorder fall back to a styled SVG still.
  • We evaluated GIF and rejected it: for this content a GIF is 10-30 MB of 256-color banding; every target platform prefers MP4.

08 / 08

→ REAL, SIMULATED, OPEN

Real today

  • Full nine-beat scroll experience, desktop + mobile
  • Tap gate, hold-to-light, ignition cinematic
  • Live shared galaxy (Supabase), realtime, personal light links
  • Recorded share video + native share sheet
  • Live counter with milestone behavior
  • Reduced-motion pathway throughout
  • Deterministic, reproducible scene

Simulated or absent

  • Payment. The gift path simulates; no money moves. We can wire Stripe Checkout + webhook → ignition ourselves if that helps the budget (as offered in the brief).
  • Explorable topography. Not built; this is the new layer the brief asks you to design.
  • Calm mode. Only the reduced-motion floor exists, not the designed on-entry choice.
  • Older-phone validation. Tuned for modern phones; the low-end matrix is untested.
  • No CMS, no i18n, no email capture; all deliberate prototype scope.

Honest edges we already know about

  • WebM downloads on desktop Chrome can't be AirDropped into an iOS photo library (the mobile share-sheet path covers the case that matters; ffmpeg.wasm or server-side transcode would close it fully).
  • The scene graph couples the traveling light and name tags to the galaxy's scroll-driven rotation: anything you add that rides a branch must be parented to the galaxy object, not the scene.
  • Long-press on iOS needs the text-selection callout suppressed on the hold button (done in CSS, deserves a real-device pass).
  • The mobile form vs. the virtual keyboard: unsolved. When the keyboard opens over the floating form, the visual viewport, the fixed WebGL scene, and the scroll position fight: content jumps above/below the form, before and after typing. iOS Safari is the hard case; it needs deliberate visual-viewport choreography or a rethought mobile input pattern, not a patch.
